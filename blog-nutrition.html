Nutrition Guide

Posted on May 30, 2024

A healthy horse starts with a healthy diet. Proper nutrition is the fuel for performance, the base for health, and the secret to a shiny coat.

Feeding your horse isn't just about providing energy; it's about meeting complex physiological needs that vary based on age, workload, and metabolism. This guide covers the fundamentals of equine nutrition.

Forage First

The foundation of every horse's diet should be high-quality forage - either pasture or hay. Horses are trickle feeders, and their digestive systems are designed to process small amounts of fiber continuously. Ideally, a horse should consume 1.5% to 2% of their body weight in forage daily.

Meeting Energy Requirements

While forage provides the bulk of the diet, performance horses or those with high metabolisms may need supplemental energy from grains (concentrates) or fats. It's important to match the energy intake to the horse's activity level to maintain an ideal body condition score.

The Role of Vitamins and Minerals

Even the best hay can be deficient in certain key nutrients depending on where it was grown. A balanced vitamin and mineral supplement ensure that your horse's metabolic processes function correctly and support hoof health, immune function, and coat condition.

Essential Hydration

Water is the most critical nutrient of all. A horse can survive for weeks without food but only a few days without water. Ensure your horse has access to clean, fresh water at all times. On average, a horse will drink 5 to 10 gallons of water a day, more if they are working hard or in hot weather.

Feeding Rules of Thumb

  • Feed small meals often rather than one or two large ones.
  • Make changes to the diet gradually over 7 to 10 days.
  • Always have fresh water available.
  • Feed by weight, not by volume (use a scale for hay and grain).
  • Wait at least an hour after a hard workout before feeding grain.

Consult with an equine nutritionist or your veterinarian to develop a customized feeding plan that meets your horse's specific needs. A well-fed horse is a happy, healthy, and high-performing partner.
