Winter Care Essentials

Posted on August 15, 2024

As temperatures drop, your horse's care routine needs to adapt. Here's everything you need to know to keep your equine partner healthy and comfortable through the winter months.

Winter presents unique challenges for horse owners, from managing blankets to ensuring proper hydration. With the right preparation and knowledge, you can help your horse thrive during the colder season.

Blanketing Basics

Not all horses need blankets, but many benefit from them during extreme cold. Consider your horse's body condition, coat thickness, and living conditions. A horse in good condition with a full winter coat and access to shelter may not need a blanket until temperatures drop below 20Â°F.

If you do blanket, ensure proper fit and check daily for rubs or shifting. Have multiple weights available for varying temperatures, and always remove wet blankets immediately.

Hydration is Critical

Horses drink less in winter, which can lead to impaction colic. Ensure water is never frozen and consider using heated buckets or tank heaters. Some horses prefer slightly warm water in winter.

Monitor water intake closely - an average horse should drink 5-10 gallons daily, even in cold weather. Adding electrolytes to feed can encourage drinking.

Nutrition Adjustments

Horses burn more calories staying warm in winter. Increase hay rations by 10-20% during cold snaps. The fermentation process of digesting hay actually helps generate body heat.

Quality forage is more important than grain for warmth. Consider adding a fat supplement for horses who need extra calories without excess sugar.

Hoof Care

Ice and snow can pack into hooves, creating dangerous "snowballs" that affect balance. Apply hoof oil or petroleum jelly to prevent snow accumulation. Consider snow pads if your horse is shod.

Maintain regular farrier visits - hooves still grow in winter and proper balance is crucial on icy ground.

Exercise and Turnout

Don't stop exercising just because it's cold! Horses need movement year-round. Warm up slowly and cool down thoroughly. A warm, damp horse can chill quickly in cold air.

Turnout is still important for mental and physical health. Clear snow from paddocks when possible and provide windbreaks.
